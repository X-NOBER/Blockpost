Chapter One

The air in the Silent City carries the weight of two thousand years of secrets, pressed thin like dried flowers between the pages of a forgotten hymnal. I sat cross-legged on the cold granite floor of my father’s private study, a space carved so deep into the marrow of the mountains that the world above—the world of sunlight, screaming dragons, and bloodied steel—felt like a fever dream.

For twenty years, this subterranean silence had been my armor. My father, the Head Librarian of the Nephilim, had raised me among the whispers of dead poets and the logic of ancient tacticians. While other children were learning to draw the Iratze to heal their scrapes, I was learning the linguistic roots of the High Runes. While they were training with wooden staves in the sparring rings of Alicante, I was mapping the migration patterns of the Great Void Demons that had haunted Idris since the first age.

I ran my fingers over the gold-leafed title of The Fall of the First Peaks. It was a book I had read so many times the spine was held together by little more than prayer and a few temporary Adhesive runes I’d etched onto the leather. My father had always told me that history was the only thing more permanent than a dragon’s fire. He’d say that a rider’s flame could burn a city to ash in a heartbeat, but a scribe’s ink could decide how that fire was remembered for a millennium.

But my father was dead. He had died in these very stacks, leaving me with a library full of knowledge and a body that was essentially a structural disaster. To the Silent Brothers—those eyeless, telepathic monks who served as our archivists and healers—I was a medical curiosity. They called it a "malady of the connective tissue," a byproduct of a childhood fever that had turned my ligaments into wet lace and my bones into porous stone. In the world of Shadowhunters, where we were supposed to be the perfected children of the Angel Raziel, I was a defect.

“You’re lingering, Elara.”

The voice didn't just break the silence; it shattered it like a hammer to glass. I didn't need to look up to know it was my mother, General Lilith Sorrengail. Her presence always brought a sudden, violent drop in the temperature of the room. She stood in the arched doorway, her silhouette framed by the flickering, spectral blue of the witch-light torches in the hallway.

She was in full Skystriker regalia—charcoal-black reinforced leathers that didn't just fit her; they seemed to be a part of her skin. The heavy silver wings pinned to her chest caught the torchlight, glinting like the bared teeth of a predator. At her hips sat two daggers made of Adamas—the heavenly silver forged from the Mortal Cup’s heat.

“I was checking the binding on the annals,” I said, my voice sounding thin and brittle. I stood up slowly, bracing myself. My knees gave a sharp, sickening pop as I straightened, a jolt of white-hot pain blooming in my hips. I swallowed it down. In this house, pain was a weakness to be exploited, not a symptom to be treated. My mother’s gaze never softened; it only sharpened, as if she were trying to find a way to forge me into something useful.

“The annals will be here when you return, or they will be ash if the wards fall,” she said, her voice a low, dangerous vibration. “The Venin have allied with Greater Demons from the Void, Elara. They aren't just raiding the borders anymore. They are draining the ley lines of Idris, turning the outer forests into a wasteland of Forsaken. We need riders. We need bodies in the sky. We don’t need another girl who knows how to conjugate dead languages.”

“I have a twenty-percent chance of surviving the Parapet, Mother,” I reminded her. I had calculated it based on my weight, the average wind shear at the Basgiath peaks, and the friction coefficient of wet limestone. “My ankles will snap under the weight of a standard-issue rucksack. If the wind catches me on that three-foot-wide stone, I won't even have the chance to fall—the pressure differential alone will peel me off the ridge like a loose leaf.”

“Then you had better find a dragon who doesn't mind a rider made of glass,” she snapped. She walked over, her heavy boots thudding against the stone, and gripped my chin. Her hand was calloused and smelled of dragon-fire and ozone. “Or you had better learn to etch runes that can hold you together when your body fails. You have your father’s constitution, but you have my blood. And my blood does not hide in a library while the world burns.”

She shoved a silver Stele into my hand. It was heavier than the scholarly one I used, built for the rapid-fire combat marks needed in the field. This wasn't for recording history; it was for surviving it.

“The carriage leaves in ten minutes. If you aren't in it, the Peacekeepers will drag you to the base of the mountain in chains. A Sorrengail enters the War College on her feet, or she doesn't enter at all.”

She turned on her heel and left, her cape snapping behind her like the wings of a predatory bird. No "happy birthday." No "stay alive." Just the cold, rhythmic click of her boots echoing down the hallway until the silence swallowed it whole.

I looked down at the Stele in my hand. It was a masterpiece of Adamas work, etched with the patterns of the first Skystrikers. On my bed lay the flight suit—a sleek, black suit of armor that felt like a funeral shroud. I reached into my desk drawer and pulled out the only thing I truly owned: a dragon-scale dagger my brother, Brennan, had given me before he vanished in the Great Uprising. The scales were obsidian, humming with a low-frequency vibration that made my teeth ache. Brennan had been a rider. He had been strong. He had been everything my mother wanted, and the mountain had swallowed him anyway.

I pulled on the leathers, the material stiff and unforgiving. I began to etch. I wasn't just drawing runes; I was stitching myself together. I drew the Stamina rune over my heart, the Fortitude mark on my sternum, and a series of Binding runes on my ankles and knees. The silver ink hissed as it sank into my skin, pulling the necessary energy straight from my marrow. It was an agonizing process—every rune felt like a hot coal being pressed into my flesh—but I needed the artificial stability. Without these marks, I wouldn't make it to the carriage, let alone the Parapet.

I stood in front of the mirror, a girl made of ink and borrowed strength. I looked at the rucksack sitting by the door, stuffed with the heavy technical manuals I refused to leave behind. If I was going to die, I was going to die with the truth in my bag.

As I walked out of the library, the scent of old paper was replaced by the ozone of a brewing storm. The carriage was waiting in the courtyard of the Silent City, its black horses restless in the grey morning light. My sister, Mira—already a decorated rider—stood by the door, her expression a mix of terror and pride.

“Don’t let them see you shake, Elara,” she whispered, pressing a small vial of poppy tincture into my hand. “And when you get to the bridge... don't look at the sky. Look at the stone.”

I nodded, my throat too tight to speak. I climbed into the carriage, the door slamming shut with the finality of a prison cell. I was twenty years old. I was a Scribe. And as the carriage began its climb toward the Basgiath peaks, I realized that I wasn't just going to a college. I was going to a graveyard.

The peaks rose before us, jagged and black against the bruised sky. Somewhere up there, the dragons were waiting. And somewhere up there, Xaden Herondale—the man whose father my mother had executed—was waiting too.

I gripped my Stele until my knuckles turned white. If the world wanted to break me, it was going to have to work for it.

Chapter Two

The carriage ride to the base of the Basgiath peaks was a descent into a specific kind of hell. For three days, I was trapped in a small, rattling wooden box with two other candidates: Rhiannon, a girl from a minor Shadowhunter house who possessed a smile that felt like sunlight, and Jack Havoc, a boy whose name was as subtle as his personality. Jack spent the entire journey sharpening a massive broadsword and explaining, in exhaustive detail, exactly how he intended to "subdue" a Red Daggertail dragon.

I didn't talk much. I couldn't afford to. Every ounce of my energy was directed toward the runes etched into my skin. The Fortitude mark on my sternum was a constant, dull throb, siphoning my stamina just to keep my spine straight. I felt like a clockwork doll held together by fraying silver wire.

When the carriage finally lurched to a halt at the foot of the college, the sheer, vertical reality of my situation hit me.

The Alicante War College wasn't just built on the mountain; it was fused to it. It was a fortress of obsidian and white stone, jagged and hostile, perched on peaks that seemed to pierce the very belly of the clouds. To reach the entrance, we had to ascend a winding, narrow staircase carved into the cliffside—thousands of steps that felt like a deliberate attempt to kill the weak before they even reached the real test.

By the time I reached the summit, my lungs were burning and my Binding runes were flickering a dangerous, pale grey. I stood in a line with several hundred other candidates, all of us drenched by a freezing rain that smelled of ozone and ancient magic.

And then, I saw the Parapet.

It was a thousand-foot-long bridge of limestone, barely three feet wide, connecting the outer watchtower to the main gates of the college. There were no railings. There was no safety net. There was only a five-hundred-foot drop into a canyon filled with jagged granite and the churning white water of the Acheron River.

“Candidate 114! Advance!” the commandant barked.

I watched as the boy in front of me—a tall, muscular candidate who had made it up the stairs without breaking a sweat—stepped onto the stone. He took five confident strides. Then, the wind shrieked. It was a localized gale, a literal wall of air that slammed into the side of the bridge. He didn't even have time to scream. One moment he was a person; the next, he was a black speck tumbling into the mist.

The line went dead silent. The rain felt colder.

"Sorrengail!" the commandant yelled. "Move or be disqualified."

I adjusted the straps of my rucksack. It was heavy with the books I refused to leave behind, but I’d balanced the weight meticulously. I pulled my Stele from my belt and touched it to the Balance runes on my ankles. They flared a desperate, blinding blue.

I stepped onto the stone.

The first sensation was the vibration. The bridge wasn't static; it hummed with the resonance of the wind and the distant roars of the dragons in the upper peaks. I didn't look down. I couldn't. If I saw the void, my brain would override the runes and send me into a spiral of vertigo. I focused on the stone six inches in front of my boots.

One. Two. Three.

The wind clawed at my flight suit, trying to find a grip. Every muscle in my body was locked in a state of high-tension terror. My joints felt like they were being pried apart by invisible levers. Ten feet in, a gust slammed into my left shoulder. I swayed, my boot skidding on a patch of slick moss.

My heart hammered—thump-crack, thump-crack—as I dropped to one knee. The impact sent a jolt of agony through my patella that made my vision swim with white spots.

"Don't stop," I whispered to myself, the rain stinging my eyes. "If you stop, you're a statue. If you're a statue, you're a target."

I forced myself back up, the Fortitude mark on my chest burning so hot I could smell the faint scent of singed skin. I was halfway across when the shadows began to change.

They weren't moving with the clouds. They were detached. At the far end of the bridge, a figure stood silhouetted against the dark obsidian archway of the college. He was lean, dressed in charcoal-black leathers that seemed to drink the rain. As I drew closer, the details sharpened.

Xaden Herondale.

I knew the name from my mother’s maps of the Great Uprising. I knew the face from the sketches in the Scribe files—the "Traitor's Son" who had been spared only because the dragons had chosen him. He stood with his arms crossed, watching my agonizing progress with a look of cold, clinical detachment.

"You're late, Sorrengail," he called out. His voice was a low rumble that somehow carried perfectly through the howling wind.

I didn't answer. I couldn't breathe, let alone speak. I was twenty feet from the end when the bridge gave a sickening groan. A section of the limestone edge crumbled away, taking the space where my next step should have been.

I lunged forward, my fingers clawing at the rough stone. I was dangling off the edge, my legs swinging over five hundred feet of nothingness. My rucksack pulled at my shoulders, the weight of the books suddenly feeling like a literal anchor to my grave.

"Let the bag go," Xaden said. He was standing right at the edge now, looking down at me. His eyes were a terrifying mix of onyx and gold, shifting like a storm.

"No," I gasped, my fingers slipping.

"It's paper, Elara. It’s not worth your life."

"It's history," I bit out, find a finger-hold in a crack in the stone. "And I'm a Scribe."

I hauled myself up, my Binding runes screaming in protest as they held my joints together under the impossible strain. I rolled onto the solid obsidian floor of the courtyard, gasping for air, the rain washing the copper taste of blood from my mouth.

I looked up. Xaden was standing over me. Up close, the air around him was different—heavy, charged with the static of the Void. On his neck, the "Mark of the Traitor" swirled like a living thing, a jagged, obsidian tattoo that marked him as the son of a rebel.

"You have a lot of heart for a girl made of glass," he said, his voice dropping to a dangerous whisper. He reached down and gripped my chin, much like my mother had, but his touch didn't feel like ice. It felt like a spark. "But heart doesn't keep you alive when Sgaeyl gets hungry. And my dragon is very hungry today."

He looked at the dragon-scale dagger in my boot—the one with the Herondale crest. His eyes narrowed, a flash of pure, unadulterated hatred flickering in the gold. "Where did you get that?"

"It was my brother's," I said, meeting his gaze.

Xaden’s jaw tightened, the shadows at his feet lashing out like whips. "Brennan Sorrengail is dead. And you shouldn't be carrying the steel of a family your mother destroyed."

He let go of my chin, stepping back into the darkness of the archway. "Welcome to Fourth Wing, Elara. Try not to die in your sleep. My shadows don't like the smell of rotting Scribes."

I stood up, my body shaking, and watched him walk away. I had survived the Parapet. But as I looked at the dark fortress surrounding me, I realized the bridge was the easy part.

Chapter Three

The walk from the obsidian courtyard to the Fourth Wing barracks was a slow-motion descent into agony. Every step I took felt like a betrayal. My Binding runes, which had held my joints together during the literal life-or-death struggle on the Parapet, were now fading into a dull, ashen grey. Without their constant, magical reinforcement, my ligaments felt like frayed rope. My hips ground together with a sickening, audible click with every second step, and a sharp, white-hot needle of pain shot up my spine every time my boot struck the stone floor.

I followed the line of bedraggled, shivering candidates through the jagged maw of the fortress. The walls of Alicante War College weren't just stone; they were warded obsidian, etched with silver veins of Adamas that hummed with a low, oppressive frequency. To a Scribe, the architecture was a marvel of ancient engineering. To a cadet who could barely stand, it was a tomb.

"Keep moving, Sorrengail," a voice hissed behind me. It was Jack Havoc. He had made it across the bridge—likely through sheer, pig-headed luck—and he was currently vibrating with an aggressive energy that suggested he wanted to hit something. "You're clogging the artery."

I didn't give him the satisfaction of a response. I just adjusted the straps of my rucksack, which now felt like it was filled with lead ingots rather than books, and kept my eyes on the back of Rhiannon’s head.

The Fourth Wing barracks were located in the lowest, dampest quadrant of the fortress, tucked into the shadow of the dragon stalls. The air here was different—thicker, smelling of sulfur, wet leather, and the unmistakable metallic tang of blood. We were led down a spiraling staircase that seemed to drop into the very roots of the mountain.

"These are your cells," a Second-Year corporal announced, gesturing to a long hallway of arched stone openings. "They are not rooms. They are cages. You will live here, you will bleed here, and most of you will die here. There are no locks. In the Fourth Wing, if you can’t defend your own door, you don’t deserve the air you’re breathing."

I found my assignment: Cell 4-B. It was a six-by-nine space with a narrow cot, a stone basin, and a single wooden desk. I slumped against the doorframe, my breath coming in ragged gasps. The moment I crossed the threshold, the last of my Fortitude rune flickered out.

I collapsed onto the cot, my legs finally giving way. The pain wasn't just a sensation anymore; it was a physical weight, pressing me into the thin mattress. I reached for my Stele with fingers that shook so violently I nearly dropped it. I needed to etch a Healing mark, but my energy reserves were dangerously low. Drawing a rune was a transaction—the magic provided the effect, but the user provided the fuel.

I just need a minute, I thought, closing my eyes. Just one minute of silence.

But silence was a luxury the Fourth Wing didn't provide.

A shadow moved in the corner of my eye. I didn't hear a footstep. I didn't hear the rustle of fabric. I only felt the sudden, unnatural chill that swept through the room, turning my breath into a faint mist.

I lunged for the dragon-scale dagger in my boot, but before my hand could even touch the hilt, a ribbon of solid darkness lashed out from the floor. It wrapped around my wrist like a cold iron manacle and pinned my arm to the stone wall behind the cot.

"A Scribe who can't even sense a shadow shouldn't be holding a Herondale blade."

Xaden Herondale stepped out of the darkness of the corner. He didn't come through the door; he simply manifested from the gloom, the shadows peeling off his shoulders like a discarded cloak. He stood over me, his presence swallowing the meager light of the hallway torches.

"Get... out," I wheezed, my heart hammering against my ribs.

"I told you on the bridge, Sorrengail," Xaden said, his voice a low, terrifyingly calm vibration. He leaned down, placing his free hand on the mattress next to my head. He was so close I could see the golden flecks in his onyx eyes, shifting like embers in a dying fire. "My shadows don't need an invitation. And right now, they're telling me that you’re about three seconds away from a total physical collapse."

He looked down at my wrist, where the shadow-bind was holding me. His gaze shifted to the fading, messy lines of my Binding runes. "Your runework is sloppy. You’re over-etching the secondary lines, wasting energy you don't have. If you keep drawing them like this, you’ll burn out your marrow before the first week of training is over."

"I don't remember asking for a tutor," I spat, though my voice lacked any real bite.

Xaden’s eyes narrowed. The "Mark of the Traitor" on his neck pulsed with a dull, obsidian light. "You didn't. But you’re in my wing. If you fail, it’s a stain on my record. And if you die because you’re too stubborn to admit your body is breaking, it’s a waste of my time."

He reached out with his other hand. For a terrifying second, I thought he was going to choke me. Instead, his fingers brushed against the Fortitude rune on my collarbone. His touch wasn't cold like the shadows; it was searingly hot, like a brand.

"Your mother killed my father for a rebellion he believed in," Xaden whispered, his face inches from mine. "She branded every one of us—the children of the traitors—with these marks. She thought it would ensure our loyalty. She thought the dragons would burn the treason out of us."

He let go of my wrist, and the shadows retracted instantly, pooling at his feet like loyal hounds. He stood up, looking down at me with a mixture of hatred and something I couldn't quite identify—something that looked dangerously like curiosity.

"But the dragons didn't burn it out," he continued. "They amplified it. We are the most powerful riders this college has seen in a century, and we are all under your mother’s thumb. Do you have any idea how much my people want to see a Sorrengail bleed?"

"I imagine about as much as you do," I said, rubbing my sore wrist.

"More," Xaden said. "I want to see if you're actually as fragile as you look, or if you're just another lie your mother told the world. Sleep with your eyes open, Elara. The 'Traitor’s Children' are hungry, and you look like a feast."

He turned and walked toward the doorway, but paused at the threshold. "And Sorrengail? Use more silver ink on the Stamina anchor. If you're going to cheat death, at least do it with some dignity."

He vanished into the hall, the shadows following him like a living cape.

I stayed on the cot for a long time, the silence of the room feeling heavier than before. My wrist was still cold where the shadow had touched it, but my collarbone—where his fingers had brushed the rune—was burning.

I picked up my Stele and began to etch, this time following his advice. I reinforced the anchors, deepened the silver lines, and felt the energy settle into my joints with a new, terrifyingly efficient precision.

I was a Scribe. I was a Shadowhunter. And I was trapped in a room with a man who had every reason to kill me, yet had just shown me how to survive.

As the distant roar of a dragon echoed through the mountain, I realized that the Parapet was just the beginning. The real war wasn't in the sky; it was in the shadows of the Fourth Wing.

Chapter Four

The silence that followed Xaden’s departure was a vacuum, a hollow space filled only by the rhythmic, distant thrum of the mountain’s heart—the collective breathing of hundreds of dragons perched in the heights above.

I lay on my back, staring at the uneven obsidian ceiling of my cell. Every breath felt like a labor, my ribs aching where the rucksack straps had bruised the skin. The Healing rune I had just etched—using Xaden’s unsolicited advice—was working differently than my previous marks. Usually, my runes felt like a frantic bandage, a temporary hold against the inevitable collapse. This one felt like a deep, slow heat, radiating from the bone outward. It didn't take away the pain, but it made the pain feel… manageable.

It was a terrifying realization: Xaden Herondale, the man who should want me dead more than anyone in Idris, knew my body’s limitations better than I did.

Don’t think about him, I told myself, clutching the dragon-scale dagger to my chest. Think about the morning. Think about the first bell.

But sleep was a ghost. My mind kept drifting to the "Mark of the Traitor." I had seen the files in the Silent City archives—the redacted reports from the Great Uprising. When the rebellion was crushed, my mother hadn’t just executed the parents; she had branded the children. Every child of a rebel was forced to witness their parents' deaths and then subjected to the "Binding of the Traitor"—a magical brand that ensured they would serve the Skystrikers as frontline fodder. It was a death sentence draped in the guise of a second chance.

Xaden was the first of them. The leader of the "Children of the Damned." And I was the daughter of their executioner, sleeping in a room without a lock.

A sudden, sharp clang echoed through the stone corridor—the sound of a heavy iron bar being struck against the cell doors.

"Roll call! Out! Now!" a voice bellowed.

I rolled off the cot, my joints screaming a protest that I ignored with practiced ease. My Binding runes flared, locking my ankles into place as I pulled on my heavy combat boots. I didn't have time to look in the mirror; I grabbed my Stele and stepped out into the hallway.

The Fourth Wing cadets were a sea of black leather and bruised faces. We were lined up against the cold stone walls, our breaths hitching in the frigid subterranean air. To my left, Rhiannon stood with her jaw set, a jagged cut above her eyebrow from the Parapet still weeping blood. To my right stood a boy I didn't recognize—thin, pale, with a "Mark of the Traitor" that snaked up his neck like a blackened vine.

At the end of the hall stood the Second-Year who had led us here: a tall, cruel-faced Shadowhunter named Vane.

"You think you’re cadets because you survived the bridge?" Vane sneered, walking down the line. He stopped in front of a girl three doors down and backhanded her across the face. She didn't flinch, though her lip split instantly. "You’re nothing. You’re meat. The dragons haven't looked at you yet. Until they do, you are the property of the Fourth Wing."

He stopped in front of me. His eyes—a dull, muddy brown—raked over my frame with a sneer. "And you. The General’s little doll. I heard you almost fell twice."

"Almost doesn't count in history, Corporal," I said, my voice steadier than I felt.

Vane leaned in, his breath smelling of stale ale and tobacco. "In history? No. But in the pits, 'almost' is just a slow way to die." He looked at the Stamina rune on my collarbone. "You’re cheating, Sorrengail. Using runes to augment a body that clearly should have stayed in the nursery. If I catch you using a Stele during the combat trials, I’ll break your fingers one by one."

"Runes are legal until the Threshing," Rhiannon interrupted, her voice clear and defiant. "It’s in the Codex."

Vane turned his predatory gaze on her. "The Codex doesn't apply in the dark, Blackthorn. But if you want to defend her, you can join her for the midnight scour."

He turned back to the line. "Listen up. The ley lines are thinning. The wards at the southern border are failing because we don’t have enough Skystrikers to fuel the Adamas stones. That means your training is being accelerated. Tomorrow morning, you don't go to the classroom. You go to the Gauntlet. If you don't finish the course in under ten minutes, you don't eat. If you don't finish it at all… the dragons get their morning exercise."

He struck the iron bar against the wall one last time, the sound vibrating in my teeth. "Back in your holes. Lights out in five minutes."

The line broke, and we scrambled back into our cells. As I turned to enter mine, I felt a hand on my arm.

It was the boy with the Traitor’s Mark from the line. He looked at me with an expression that wasn't hatred, but a strange, hollow pity.

"Don't let Vane see the Stele again," he whispered, his voice barely a breath. "He’s a Herondale loyalist. He wants to be the one to give Xaden your head."

"Why are you telling me this?" I asked.

The boy touched the jagged mark on his neck. "Because Xaden said you were off-limits until the Threshing. And Xaden doesn't like it when people touch his things."

He vanished into his cell before I could respond.

I retreated into my room, my heart hammering. His things. Xaden didn't see me as a person; he saw me as a political piece on a board. A Sorrengail to be used, protected, or discarded when the time was right.

I sat on my cot and pulled out a small piece of parchment and a charcoal pencil. I couldn't sleep, so I did the only thing that made sense. I began to map the Gauntlet from memory. I had read the tactical diagrams in my father’s office. I knew the height of the walls, the depth of the mud pits, and the frequency of the swinging iron pendulums.

I spent hours in the dark, my fingers moving in a blur. I wasn't training my muscles; I was training my mind. I was finding the shortcuts. I was calculating the exact moment my Balance rune would need the most energy.

I was about to finish the map when a single shadow detached itself from the wall near my desk. It didn't take the shape of a man, but it moved with an intentionality that made my hair stand on end. It hovered over my drawing for a second, almost as if it were reading the notes, before it dissipated into the floor.

He was watching. He was always watching.

I blew out the candle and lay down in the dark, gripping my dagger. I was Elara Sorrengail. I was twenty years old. I was made of glass and ink and borrowed fire. And tomorrow, I was going to show Xaden Herondale that a Scribe can be more dangerous than a soldier.

Chapter Five

The morning bell screamed. It was a jagged, bronze sound that tore through the thin veil of my restless sleep, vibrating against the stone walls of my cell until my very marrow seemed to hum in protest.

I was awake before the second strike. In the Fourth Wing, being caught in your cot after the bell was an invitation for a corporal to find a "creative" way to motivate you. My body felt like a rusted machine. The Healing rune Xaden had helped me fix had settled into a dull, low-thrumming ache, but the moment I swung my legs over the side of the cot, a sharp, familiar bolt of lightning shot through my left hip.

Move, I commanded myself. Pain is just data. Process it and move.

I dressed in silence, the ritual of the flight leathers becoming a sort of meditation. Boots laced tight to support my weak ankles. Dagger secured to the inner thigh. Stele hidden in the secret pocket of my sleeve. I didn't reach for the Stele yet; siphoning energy for runes too early would leave me hollow by the time I actually reached the Gauntlet.

The hallway was already a chaotic river of black-clad bodies. The smell was a suffocating mix of unwashed sweat, damp wool, and the underlying, omnipresent scent of dragon sulfur that clung to the very pores of the fortress.

"You look like you've seen a ghost, Sorrengail," a voice chirped beside me.

It was Rhiannon. She looked remarkably put-together for someone who had spent the night on a stone slab. Her dark hair was braided tight against her scalp, and she was already chewing on a piece of dried fruit she’d likely scavenged.

"I spent the night dreaming of ways to gravity-proof my joints," I muttered, falling into step with her as we headed toward the mess hall.

"Better than my night," she sighed, her expression darkening. "The girl in the cell next to mine—Tessa—she didn't make it through the night. Not a traitor’s child, just a regular cadet. Someone went in with a garrote around midnight. No one heard a thing."

I felt a cold shiver that had nothing to do with the drafty corridors. "Was it a grudge?"

"In here? It’s all a grudge," Rhiannon said, lowering her voice as we entered the massive, vaulted dining hall. "But word is, she was bragging about having a high affinity for Green Daggertails. Someone wanted to thin the competition before the Threshing."

The dining hall was a sea of long, obsidian tables. It was the only place where the different wings mixed, though the lines were clearly drawn. At the center, under the massive hanging banners of the Shadowhunter houses, sat the Third-Years—the elite. They ate in relative silence, their movements graceful and predatory. Further out were the Second-Years, and at the very edges, huddled like sheep in a wolf den, were the First-Year cadets.

We grabbed our trays—metal plates holding a greyish porridge that smelled of oats and iron—and found a spot at the end of a Fourth Wing table.

"Don't eat the bread," a voice whispered from across the table.

I looked up. It was the pale boy from the night before—the one who had warned me about Vane. Up close, his "Mark of the Traitor" was even more intricate, a swirling pattern of thorns that disappeared beneath his collar.

"Why not?" Rhiannon asked, her hand hovering over a small, hard roll.

"Laxatives," the boy said, not looking up from his bowl. "The Second-Years like to see who has the stomach for the Gauntlet. Half those rolls are spiked. If you’re cramping on the swinging poles, you’re dead."

I could feel the heat radiating off its scales the way you feel a hearth fire from across a room. Its white eyes, each one larger than my entire body, were absolutely, terrifyingly calm.

In my chest, the Adamas-shard pulsed once. Twice. Then it stopped pulsing and began to glow—steady and constant, the way a star glows, without urgency, without fluctuation.

"Elara." Xaden's voice was very quiet. "What is it doing?"

"I think," I said, with the careful, measured diction of someone who has spent their entire life in archives and knows precisely how important it is to use the correct word, "it's waiting for an introduction."

I did not reach for my Stele. I did not draw a rune. I pressed two fingers to the Adamas-shard through my leathers and I said, to the oldest living thing I had ever encountered, the only thing that felt true:

"My name is Elara Sorrengail. I'm a Scribe. I've read every history of this mountain that survived the First Age, and none of them mentioned you—which means either the historians were afraid of you, or they loved you too much to commit you to paper where the wrong eyes might find you." I held its white gaze. "I think it was the latter."

For a very long moment, nothing happened.

Then the Progenitor exhaled—a slow, vast, deliberate breath that smelled of copper and deep stone and something else, something I couldn't name but recognized in the way you recognize a piece of music you've never heard before but somehow already know. The warmth of it moved through me like a tide.

And in the cavity of my chest, where the violet-bond lived alongside the Adamas-shard and the borrowed fire of twenty etched runes, something new took root. Not a bond—not exactly. Something older than a bond. A recognition.

Above us, through the shattered ceiling of the rift, the stars were going dark one by one as the King consumed them. We had perhaps thirty-six hours before there was nothing left to save.

But the Progenitor was awake.

And it remembered what it had been sealed away to protect.

Chapter Thirty-Two

The Progenitor unfolded its wings.

The sound was not a snap or a crack. It was a pressure change—a sudden, violent redistribution of air that hit us like a wall and sent Ridoc skidding three feet across the stone before Rhiannon caught him. Each wing was a continent. That is not hyperbole; that is the only unit of measurement that applies. They filled the rift from wall to demolished wall, and when they still didn't fit, the walls simply ceased to be walls. The Progenitor's wingspan rewrote the architecture of the mountain's interior the way a tide rewrites a sandbar—completely, without malice, without noticing.

"Down!" Xaden had me by the collar of my flight leathers before I'd consciously registered the need to move. His shadows threw a ceiling over us, a low, dense vault of dark matter that absorbed the first concussive downbeat and shed it sideways into the rubble.

The Progenitor launched.

It didn't take off the way a dragon takes off—the running start, the grunt of effort, the laborious climb through the first hundred feet of altitude. It simply decided to be somewhere else, and the air rearranged itself in panicked compliance. One moment it occupied the rift. The next, a column of superheated atmosphere punched upward through six hundred feet of mountain, and the Progenitor was already clearing the peak, its white eyes fixed on the thing eating the sky.

The crater it left in the summit of Basgiath was a perfect circle, sixty feet across, edges fused to glass.

We stood in the wreckage of the rift and looked up through the hole where the mountain used to be, and we watched.

The Black Hole King was no longer on the horizon. He had crossed it. He occupied the upper atmosphere now, a darkness so total and so structured that the stars around him weren't simply absent—they were being drawn toward him in long, luminous spirals, like water finding a drain. His silhouette, if you could call a living singularity a silhouette, was roughly the shape of a dragon the way a hurricane is roughly the shape of wind. He was made of the concept of consumption wearing the body of a god.

The Progenitor hit him at the thermosphere.

We felt the collision before we saw it—a shockwave that traveled down through the atmosphere and hit the earth like a second tectonic event, knocking us off our feet. The rift walls shed three feet of stone. Brennan went down hard, catching himself on both palms, silver eyes blazing.

When I looked up again, the sky was on fire.

Not orange fire. Not the violet-and-laser light of the Alpha's combat mode. White fire—the same sourceless, magnesium-white as the Progenitor's eyes—erupting in cascading detonations across the upper atmosphere as the two beings grappled. The King's darkness swallowed the fire and the Progenitor simply made more, pouring it out in sustained, structural columns that weren't burning so much as insisting. Where the King's gravity bent light inward, the Progenitor's fire bent it back out again. The sky between them was a war of physics.

"It's not enough," Ridoc said. His voice was flat and precise—not hopeless, just accurate. He was using the navigational compass again, watching the needle. The needle was not spinning anymore. It was pointing straight up, pulled toward the King's mass. "The Progenitor is matching him right now, but the King has been consuming stellar mass for however long he's been traveling. He's been eating stars, Elara. He has more fuel than the Progenitor has fire."

I already knew. I'd known the moment I watched the first white column get swallowed and not returned.

I looked at the Adamas pillar. The half-finished Supernova-Rune still glowed on its face, suspended at the edge of the chasm, intact and waiting. We'd abandoned it when the ground dropped out. We'd been thirty minutes from completing it when Aretia cracked open.

Thirty minutes.

He checked the swinging, half-melted line. "Forty percent. Maybe thirty-five. I lost some when it destabilized."

A pause. The kind of pause that means he's doing the math and not liking the answer. "To draw it, yes. To fire it—" He shook his head. "The Supernova-Rune needs a delivery mechanism, Elara. We built it to be fed into the King via the ley line network. But the ley lines aren't the problem anymore. He's not on the ground. He stopped.

We all stopped.

I turned and looked at the hole in the summit. At the white fire blooming against the dark, sixty miles above us.

"We don't need a ley line," I said. "We need a courier."

The bond was still there. Thinner than it should have been at this distance—the Progenitor was far enough away now that the new recognition-thread in my chest felt like a wire pulled to its maximum extension, humming with tension. But it was there. I could feel it.

I pressed my fingers to the Adamas-shard.

"Advast," I said. "I need you to talk to it."

His response was immediate and uneasy. Wildfire, I am a void-dragon, not a—

"I know what you are. I'm not asking you to bond with it. I'm asking you to translate." I reached deeper, past the violet filament of our bond, toward the newer thread. The recognition. The thing the Progenitor had planted in my chest when it exhaled. "It put something in me. I can feel it. I just can't speak its language."

A long silence from above. Then, from Advast, something I had never felt through the bond before: reluctance edging toward awe.

The old language, he said. I know only fragments. What do you want to tell it?

I looked at the Supernova-Rune. At my Stele. At the half-finished geometry of the most dangerous mark I'd ever attempted to draw, now glowing patiently on a pillar at the edge of the abyss.

"Tell it," I said, "that we can coat its fire."

Advast went quiet. Not the quiet of distance or interference—the quiet of someone translating very carefully.

I raised the Stele and returned to the pillar.

"Rhiannon," I said, without turning around. "I need a telekinetic brace on my left side. My hip is going." It was—the last shockwave had rattled something loose, and I could feel the joint sliding in the wrong direction with every step. I didn't have time to redraw the Binding rune. "Ridoc, I need you on the conduit. Brennan, stand ready to anchor—I'll tell you when."

"What are you doing?" Xaden had followed me to the pillar's edge. Below us, the chasm was bottomless black. Above us, the sky was a war.

"Finishing the rune," I said. "But I'm not drawing it for the ley lines anymore. I'm drawing it for her." I gestured upward, at the white fire. "The Supernova-Rune is a fractal accelerant. It takes whatever energy it's fed and collapses it toward a single infinite point. We designed it to choke the King—to give him more than his singularity could process. But it works on any energy source."

Xaden followed the logic the way he always did—fast, and without flinching from where it led. "You want to coat the Progenitor's fire with the rune. Turn every flame it breathes into a supernova charge."

"If the King tries to swallow a Supernova-Rune—"

"It collapses inward," Xaden finished. "He chokes on his own gravity." He looked up. The white fire was still holding, but the columns were getting shorter. The Progenitor was tiring. "How do we get the rune from the pillar to sixty miles up?"

I met his eyes. "The same way we get everything done."

He understood. His shadows rose without being asked—not a dome this time, not a shield, but a column, a dark spire rising from the floor of the rift and extending upward through the shattered mountain and into the open air above. A conduit of his own. A shadow-bridge from the Adamas pillar to the upper atmosphere.

"I can hold it for eight minutes," he said. His jaw was set. The Mark of the Traitor on his neck had gone pale, which meant he was already burning through his reserves. "After that, the distance is too much."

"Six," I said. "I only need six."

I pressed my Stele to the Adamas pillar and I drew.

This is what the archive records get wrong about rune-etching under pressure: they describe it as a discipline of stillness, of careful, meditative precision. That is true in a classroom. In a rift chamber with a disintegrating hip and a pillar suspended over an infinite drop and sixty miles of air between you and the thing you're trying to save, it is something else entirely. It is controlled desperation. It is the specific, furious calm of someone who has spent twenty years being told they are too fragile for this world and has chosen, in this exact moment, to make the world adjust its expectations.

The Supernova-Rune is forty-seven strokes. I drew them in four minutes and fifty seconds.

The pillar detonated with light.

Not an explosion—a bloom, slow and total, the rune geometry spreading outward from the Adamas surface in fractal branches, each branch splitting into smaller branches until the entire pillar was a lattice of living gold. The heat was immense. I staggered back, and Rhiannon's telekinetic brace caught me, held me upright, kept my hip in its socket by sheer force of will.

"Brennan," I said. "Now."

He slammed his palms into the conduit line. The thirty-five percent reserve discharged in a single pulse—a violet-white detonation that traveled up the conduit and hit the rune-lattice and was instantly, catastrophically restructured. The Supernova geometry took the raw energy and did what it was designed to do: compressed it, fractalized it, collapsed it toward a single infinite point and then held it there, trembling at the edge of its own event horizon, waiting.

Xaden's shadow-column caught the discharge at the top of the pillar and carried it up.

We watched it rise—a thread of fractal gold climbing the shadow-bridge, ascending through the shattered summit of Basgiath and into the purple-black of the upper atmosphere, climbing toward sixty miles of altitude, toward the white fire and the darkness eating it.

"Come on," Ridoc whispered. He was gripping the broken conduit line with both hands, knuckles white, watching the thread climb. "Come on, come on, come on—"

The Progenitor saw it coming.

I felt the moment it recognized the thread through the recognition-filament in my chest—a single beat of something that wasn't surprise exactly, but was the ancient, considered equivalent of it. A creature that had not been surprised in a thousand years encountering something it had not accounted for.

Then it inhaled.

And the next column of white fire it breathed into the King was threaded through, at the quantum level, with forty-seven strokes of Supernova geometry.

The King swallowed it.

The silence lasted two full seconds.

Then the upper atmosphere turned gold.

The detonation was not loud down here. Sound doesn't carry well from sixty miles up, and what reached us was less a noise than a sensation—a pressure bloom that moved through the earth itself, through the soles of our boots and the marrow of our bones. The King's outline, that impossible lightless shape against the stars, contracted. Not slowly. Suddenly—a violent inward spasm, like a fist clenching, as his own gravity turned against him and the fractal geometry inside the fire he'd swallowed refused to be absorbed and instead kept collapsing, infinitely, recursively inward.

He was choking.

He was actually choking.

"It's working," Rhiannon said, and her voice broke on the last word. Not from fear. "Elara, it's working."

Above us, the Progenitor pressed the advantage. It was not a creature that hesitated. It poured column after column of Supernova-threaded fire into the contracting mass of the King, each one another fractal loop in the recursive collapse, each one making the next more catastrophic than the last. The gold light spread across the upper atmosphere like a second dawn—warmer than the sun, steadier than the stars, the specific color of a rune drawn in Adamas by someone who intends to mean it.

Xaden let go of the shadow-column and dropped to one knee. I was at his side before he hit the stone, one hand at his shoulder, the other checking the pulse at his throat. It was there—rapid and thin, but present.

"I'm fine," he said, in the voice of someone who is decidedly not fine but has decided not to discuss it.

"You're catastrophically not fine," I said. "Don't move."

"Are you drawing a Healing rune on my neck right now?"

"I'm drawing a Stamina rune on your neck right now. The Healing rune comes after you stop being an idiot." I pressed the Stele to his skin and etched the mark with hands that were almost steady. Almost. "You held a shadow-bridge for six minutes at sixty miles. Your reserves are empty."

"Worth it," he said, and looked up at the gold sky.

I followed his gaze.

The King was smaller. Not by much—not yet. But the contraction was ongoing, self-sustaining now, the recursive geometry of the Supernova-Rune feeding itself on his own mass. He was being made to collapse the way a star collapses: not destroyed by something outside him, but betrayed by his own weight.

The Progenitor circled him. Patient. Ancient. Waiting.

In my chest, the recognition-thread pulsed—once, warm and deliberate, the way you'd press a hand briefly to someone's shoulder to say: I see you. Well done.

I pressed two fingers to the Adamas-shard and sent back the only thing I had left to give: acknowledgment. Gratitude. The particular, wordless understanding that passes between a Scribe and the subject of the most important record she has ever witnessed.

Above us, the gold kept spreading.

Below us, the core of the mountain breathed its last reserves into the rift.

And in thirty-six hours, or thirty, or twenty—the King would finish the collapse the Progenitor had started and the Supernova-Rune had made inevitable. His singularity would swallow itself. His gravity would turn inward one final time and not come back.

We would win. We were going to win.

We just had to survive long enough to see it.

"Ridoc," I said, turning around. "How much of the conduit line is still functional?"

He looked at the melted, sparking ruin in his hands. "...I would estimate none of it."

"Good assessment." I turned to Brennan. "Then we do this the old way." I held up the Stele. "Someone find me a wall."

Chapter Thirty-Three

The King's collapse took eleven hours.

We watched all of it.

There was nothing else to do. The conduit was destroyed, the Adamas pillar had burned itself to a pale, hollow shell when the Supernova-Rune discharged, and the mountain's core was running on fumes—a low, arthritic pulse of energy that was enough to keep the remaining wards from going dark but not enough to power another rune of any meaningful scale. Brennan had tried to re-rig the fuel line twice and given up both times, his silver eyes dim with an exhaustion that went beyond the physical. Ridoc had found a section of rift wall that hadn't fully collapsed and was using it as a backrest, his head tipped up, watching the sky. Rhiannon sat beside him. Neither of them spoke.

Xaden sat with his back against the Adamas pillar's base, the Stamina rune I'd drawn on his neck doing the slow, grinding work of rebuilding what the shadow-bridge had cost him. I sat beside him. His shoulder was warm against mine. I didn't move away from it.

Above us, the gold spread.

The King's contraction was not graceful. Nothing about a singularity turning against itself is graceful—it is a catastrophic, recursive violence, each layer of collapse triggering the next, the Supernova-Rune's fractal geometry winding tighter and tighter through his mass like a thread pulled through a needle's eye that keeps getting smaller. He fought it. Of course he did. There were hours when the gold dimmed and the darkness surged back, hours when his outline expanded and the remaining stars around him bent toward him again and my chest seized with the specific terror of watching something inevitable become temporarily uncertain.

But the Progenitor was always there.

Every time the King surged, she answered. She was not fighting him with volume now—not the sustained, furious columns of the first engagement. She was precise. Economical. She had found the frequency at which his contracting mass was most unstable, the exact resonance point at which another Supernova-threaded pulse would tighten the geometry another degree, and she struck it with the patience of something that has been alive since before patience was a concept that needed a name. Every three hours. Every two. Every ninety minutes as the collapse accelerated and the intervals shortened.

She was playing him like a Scribe plays a closing argument. Methodically. Inevitably.

At hour nine, Ridoc fell asleep sitting up and no one woke him.

At hour ten, Rhiannon quietly took the damaged conduit line apart and rebuilt what she could of it with her bare hands and a set of rune-etched clamps she'd had in her field kit. It wasn't enough to power anything significant. It was something to do.

At hour eleven, the King screamed.

The sound reached us as a subsonic pulse—not heard, felt, the way you feel a thunderclap in the seconds before the noise arrives. The mountain absorbed it. The rift walls shed another thin layer of stone. Ridoc jerked awake, his hand going to his dagger on pure reflex. The gold in the upper atmosphere surged to a brilliance that turned the indigo sky white, white as the Progenitor's eyes, white as Adamas heated past its yield point—

And then the darkness was simply gone.

No explosion. No dramatic implosion visible from the ground. One moment the King occupied the upper atmosphere, a hungry and immovable absence in the architecture of the sky. The next moment he did not. The stars that had been spiraling toward him snapped back to their positions like elastic returning to rest. The gravity-distortion at the horizon dissolved. The clouds, which had been pulling northward in long, unnatural streamers for three days, drifted to a halt and then, slowly, began to move in the direction wind was supposed to move them.

The sky was just sky again.

I realized I was on my feet. I didn't remember standing. Xaden was beside me, his hand finding the back of my arm. Rhiannon and Ridoc had crossed the rift floor without appearing to walk there. Brennan stood at the edge of the chasm, looking up through the hole in the summit, his expression doing something complicated that I didn't have a name for.

"Is that—" Ridoc started.

"Yes," I said.

He made a sound that was not a word and sat back down on the rubble.

We all needed a moment. We took it.

Above us, the Progenitor descended.

She came down slowly—not the controlled plummet of a combat landing, not the efficient spiral of a dragon returning to its rider. She came down the way the last snow of winter comes down: deliberately, without hurry, as if she had all the time that remained in the world and intended to use it at whatever pace suited her. Her white eyes were dimmer than they'd been at the start. Not dark—the light was still there—but banked, the way a forge fire banks when the work is done and there's no longer a reason for full heat.

She settled into the ruins of the summit with a sound like a continent sighing.

I felt it through the recognition-filament in my chest the moment she made her decision. It wasn't a declaration—it was simply a settling, a shifting of weight that went deeper than physical. The particular quality of intention that belongs to something ancient that has completed a task it has been holding in trust for a very long time.

She was going back to sleep.

"No—" The word came out before I'd chosen it. I pressed my fingers to the Adamas-shard. "Advast, tell her she doesn't have to—"

She knows, Advast said. His voice was gentler than I'd ever heard it. She is not retreating, wildfire. She is choosing. There is a difference.

The Progenitor's head came down. Her vast, iridescent neck curved in a slow arc until her snout rested in the rubble of the summit, her white eyes level with the hole she'd torn on her way out. She looked at me through it. Eight hundred feet of mountain between us, and she looked at me with the calm, unhurried focus of something that doesn't need proximity to be present.

The recognition-filament pulsed.

Once. Warm. Final.

She says, Advast translated, very quietly, that the record is complete. That the Scribe witnessed. That this is enough.

My throat closed. I pressed my fingers harder against the shard and sent back everything I had—gratitude, acknowledgment, the wordless understanding that passes between a historian and the subject of the most important record they will ever keep. The thing the books had been too afraid to write down. The name. The fact of her.

I will write you down, I thought at her. Every word. I will make sure the next Scribe knows where to look.

Her eyes closed.

The light in them didn't go out. It simply went inward—folded back inside her the way a lamp flame bends away from an open window, present, just no longer visible. Her breathing slowed to that deep, structural rhythm I'd first felt rising from the chasm—vast and biological and geological all at once. The scales along her flanks cycled through their colors one final time, every hue the spectrum contained, and then settled on something that had no name in any language I knew: the color of the moment before.

The mountain held her.

The silence was enormous and clean.

Then Ridoc said, very quietly: "Elara."

I heard it in his voice before I turned. That specific flatness that means he has done the math and the math is bad.

The sky above the summit had changed again.

Not darkness—not the King's consuming, structural dark. Something different. A seam. A vertical tear approximately three hundred feet long running through the atmosphere directly above the Progenitor's resting body, its edges ragged and luminous, crackling with a sickly energy that I recognized from the archive descriptions of the Greater Void Demon portals, the ones the Silent Brothers had classified as catastrophic-scale and sealed the records on.

The King's collapse had left a wound in the fabric of the upper air. The Supernova-Rune had burned its way through his mass at sufficient magnitude to scar the atmosphere on the other side—and on the other side of the scar was the Void. Not the metaphorical void of empty space. The actual Void. The place the Greater Demons came from.

And they were already coming through.

They came through in silence, which was the worst part. I had always imagined Void incursion as loud—the mythology was all screaming and thunder. These made no sound at all. They poured through the seam the way smoke pours through a crack, shapeless and purposeful and vast, individual forms resolving as they descended: winged silhouettes the size of wyverns, limbed and chitinous and constructed by something that understood the concept of a body without understanding what bodies were for. They fell toward the summit in a slow, deliberate rain.

Toward her.

"They can sense her," Xaden said. His voice had gone to that particular quiet that precedes his most dangerous decisions. "She's dormant. She's the largest concentration of pure primordial energy on the surface of the planet. To them she's—"

"A feast," I finished.

They landed on her. The first wave settled across her folded wings like a tide coming in across a reef, and she didn't stir. Couldn't—or wouldn't. The sleep was too deep, or the decision had been made, or she had spent the last of what she had on the King and there was simply nothing left to spend on this.

We watched them begin to feed.

It wasn't fast. It was the most terrible thing I have ever witnessed precisely because of how slow it was—the slow, systematic dismantling of something ancient and irreplaceable, the Void Demons working with a patience that mirrored her own, as if they had learned slowness from the same epoch she had. The color began to leave her scales. Not all at once. Section by section, the iridescence fading to grey, the grey deepening toward the flat, lightless non-color of something from which all energy has been extracted.

"We have to do something," Rhiannon said. She had her telekinetic field up already, was hurling chunks of rift debris through the gap in the summit at the mass of Demons on the Progenitor's body. The debris hit them and disrupted them and they simply reformed. You cannot fight Void Demons with rocks. She knew that. She was doing it anyway. "Elara, we have to—"

"I know." I had the Stele in my hand. My hands were shaking. I looked at Brennan. "The conduit. Anything left in it?"

"Eight percent. Maybe ten."

"And the pillar?"

He looked at the hollow Adamas shell. "Dregs. What the Supernova-Rune didn't burn."

"Give me everything," I said. "Everything Rhiannon's telekinetic field can pull from the ley lines, everything the conduit has left, everything in the pillar dregs." I was already moving toward the fused-glass edge of the summit gap, toward the open air. "Xaden—"

"Already here," he said, from directly behind me.

"I need a launch platform."

He didn't ask what for. He built it—a flat shelf of compressed shadow extending out from the summit gap, solid enough to stand on, oriented directly toward the mass of Demons swarming her body. I stepped onto it. The air at summit altitude was cold and thin and smelled of ozone and the aftermath of gold fire.

The Progenitor was directly below me. I could see the grey spreading across her scales like frost across a window. I could see the seam above, the ragged luminous tear, more Demons still pressing through it, a column of Void-mass descending in an endless patient stream.

I looked at the Adamas-shard against my chest. The recognition-filament, thin and cooling now that she was deep in sleep, pulsed with the last of its warmth.

I thought: I told her I would write her down.

I thought: A Scribe witnesses. But a Sorrengail acts.

I raised the Stele.

I did not draw forty-seven strokes this time. I drew one. A single mark, the root-stroke of the Supernova geometry—the foundational line from which all forty-six others branch. On its own, in Adamas, with proper fuel, it is an incomplete rune. It doesn't fire. It just holds the potential.

But I didn't draw it in Adamas. I drew it in the Adamas-shard. I pressed the Stele directly to the stone against my heart and etched the root-stroke into the shard itself, and the shard—the fragment of heavenly silver that had been fused to the violet-bond and the recognition-filament and twenty years of borrowed strength—detonated with a light that turned my vision white.

The charge didn't go up. It went down.

It traveled the recognition-filament like lightning travels a rod—direct, instantaneous, with absolute conviction about its destination. It hit the Progenitor in the deep place where the sleep lived, in the geological core of her that was still warm, that the Void Demons hadn't reached yet, and it delivered the only message it carried:

The Scribe witnessed. Get up.

For three seconds nothing happened.

Then the color came back.

Not gradually. All at once—a single, catastrophic return of the full spectrum, every hue simultaneously, brighter than anything I had seen her produce while waking. The Void Demons on her flanks recoiled. The ones anchored to her wings were thrown clear as the wings themselves snapped open—not the slow continental unfolding of before, but a violent, incandescent detonation of wingspan that sent a shockwave across the summit rubble and knocked Ridoc and Rhiannon off their feet and broke three of my ribs on Xaden's shadow-shelf and I did not care, I did not care even slightly.

The Progenitor rose.

She rose with every Void Demon that was still clinging to her body, fifty or sixty of them, unable to detach in time, fused to her scales by the velocity of her ascent. She didn't try to shake them. She rose straight toward the seam—toward the wound in the sky they'd come through—and the Demons still streaming downward reversed, turning back toward the Void in sudden, instinctive panic, but the seam was already closing around her as she pushed through it, her body filling the tear edge to edge, the luminous crackling of the portal's margin running across her scales like current across a conductor.

She looked back.

One white eye, burning full and brilliant, visible through the closing seam for exactly the length of a single breath.

Then she went through.

The explosion came from inside the Void.

The seam slammed shut.

The sound arrived four seconds later: a compression wave that cracked the remaining rift walls, shattered the hollow Adamas pillar to powder, and knocked every one of us flat on the rubble. My broken ribs introduced themselves to the stone floor with great enthusiasm. I lay there looking up at a sky that was simply, cleanly, entirely sky—no seam, no darkness, no gold fire, no Demons, no King. Just the stars returning to their original positions, settling back into the pattern they'd held for a thousand years before something had come along and tried to eat them.

Ridoc made a sound.

Rhiannon made a sound.

Brennan sat down in the rubble and put his face in his hands.

Xaden found me in the dark and didn't say anything, just pulled me upright and held me there, his arms careful around the broken ribs, his face pressed to the top of my head.

"She chose it," I said, into his chest. My voice came out wrong—flattened, like a rune drawn without enough pressure to make the mark hold. "She woke up for eleven hours. She killed a god. And then she chose—"

"I know," he said.

"She didn't have to—"

"I know." His arms tightened. Gently. "Elara."

I stopped. I pressed my fingers to the place where the Adamas-shard had been. The shard itself was gone—spent, consumed by the root-stroke I'd etched into it. But where it had sat against my sternum, the skin was warm. Warmer than body heat. Warmer than the residual heat of a discharged rune.

The recognition-filament was gone. But it had left something behind. A kind of scar, the way important things always leave scars—not damage, just record. Proof that the contact had been real.

I pressed my palm flat against my sternum and I held it there.

The record is complete, she had said. The Scribe witnessed.

"Not yet," I said quietly, to no one in particular. To her, maybe, wherever she had gone. To the record I was already beginning to assemble in the archive of my mind, word by word, the way my father had taught me—with precision, with care, with the particular reverence owed to something true.

I was a Scribe.

I reached into my jacket with a hand that was almost steady and took out the small field-notebook I had carried through every stage of this catastrophe. It was battered. The cover was scorched. Three pages in the middle had fused together from an earlier encounter with a ley line discharge.

I opened it to the first blank page.

I wrote: The Progenitor. What the First Riders sealed beneath Aretia. What the archive was afraid to name.

I wrote: She was real.

I wrote: I was there.

Above us, the stars held their positions. The sky breathed normally. Somewhere above the treeline—above what remained of it—Advast circled in slow, patient arcs, his void-black scales catching no light at all, his presence a steady violet warmth in the bond.

Well done, wildfire, he said.

I kept writing.

Chapter Thirty-Four

The sun rose.

That sounds simple. It isn't, when you have spent the last seventy-two hours genuinely uncertain whether the sun would still be there to rise. It came up over the eastern ridge of what remained of the Basgiath peaks the way it always had—unhurried, indifferent to the catastrophe it was illuminating, painting the shattered summit in shades of amber and pale gold that had no business being as beautiful as they were.

I watched it from the rift floor, my back against Xaden's chest, his arms a careful architecture around my broken ribs. I had drawn a Healing rune on myself an hour ago, fed it from the last dregs of my own marrow since there was nothing else left to draw from, and the ribs were knitting at approximately half the speed they would have managed with a proper Adamas source. It was deeply unpleasant. I was choosing to focus on the sunrise instead.

"The wards," Rhiannon said. She was standing at the collapsed eastern wall of the rift, looking out at the valley through the gap. Her voice had the careful neutrality of someone delivering a report they haven't yet decided how to feel about. "They're still up."

"The core has enough left for the wards," Brennan said. He was sitting cross-legged on the rubble, his silver eyes half-closed, monitoring the ley line pulse the way a physician monitors a patient's heartbeat. "Barely. They'll hold for another week, maybe ten days. After that we'll need to find another Adamas source or the outer wards start failing in sequence."

"A week is enough," I said.

"Enough for what?"

"To send word. To get people here." I shifted carefully—the Healing rune protested with a sharp, specific pain that I filed away and ignored. "The Skystrikers. The Silent Brothers. The rest of the wings. The King is gone. The Void seam is closed. The wards are still standing." I paused. "Basgiath is still standing. Mostly."

Ridoc, who had been lying flat on his back on the rubble with his arms spread like a man making a stone angel, turned his head to look at me. "When you say 'mostly'—"

"The summit is gone. Three of the outer towers. The eastern residential wing." I had done a structural assessment by feel and rune-sense in the small hours of the morning, while everyone else slept and I couldn't. "The library is intact."

"Of course it is," Xaden said, very quietly, into my hair.

"The library is always intact," Ridoc agreed, in the tone of someone who has made peace with this fact about the universe.

The sun climbed. The light reached the valley floor and moved across it slowly, and what it revealed in sequence was: the blue-glass graveyard that the Neptune-God's breath had made of the lower city, still crystalline and alien in the morning light. The scorched craters from the Alpha's laser strikes. The flattened forest to the south, the trees laid flat in radiating lines from the tectonic events of the last three days. And beyond all of it—beyond the devastation, at the edges of the valley where the oldest wards ran—Idris, still green. Still breathing. Still there.

The ley lines were thin. I could feel that from here, the attenuated pulse of a network that had been systematically drained by the Venin alliance and then pushed to its limits powering the defenses. They would need months to recover. Maybe a year. But they were there, and they were moving, the slow circulatory system of a land that had survived worse than this—had survived the First Age, the Void Wars, the Great Uprising—and had quietly, stubbornly persisted.

"Someone has to go up," I said. "To the summit. To make sure she—" I stopped. Restarted. "To document the site before anyone else reaches it."

The silence that followed understood what I meant. The Progenitor was gone, but Aretia was not. The buried city was real, the chasm was real, the hollow Adamas pillar was real, and when the Skystrikers arrived in their organized, administrative efficiency, someone was going to start asking questions and drawing conclusions that would find their way into the official record before I had a chance to shape the narrative.

I was a Scribe. Shaping the narrative was my function.

"I'll go," I said.

"Your ribs—" Rhiannon started.

"Are sixty percent healed and fully functional for light activity." A generous assessment. "Brennan, I need whatever Adamas dust is left from the pillar. Even the powder will fuel the Stele for a few lines." I was already calculating—what I needed to document, in what order, with what level of precision. The chasm dimensions. The fused-glass ring of the Progenitor's launch point. The stratigraphy of the Aretia seal, visible now where the Progenitor's exit had sheared through it: three hundred feet of engineered granite, laid in deliberate courses by hands that had understood exactly what they were enclosing and had chosen to enclose it with care rather than contempt.

Brennan wordlessly produced a small vial of Adamas powder from his field kit—a rider's emergency supply, the kind everyone carried and almost no one ever used. It was enough to fuel six or seven marks. I would have to be economical.

I was always economical.

Xaden helped me to my feet. He didn't comment on the way I leaned into the assist more than I would have twenty-four hours ago; I didn't comment on the shadows moving in quiet, protective arcs around us. We had arrived, somewhere in the past seventy-two hours, at the place where we no longer needed to narrate what we were doing for each other. It had happened without announcement, the way most important things do.

"I'll come with you," he said.

"You need to rest—"

"Yes," he agreed pleasantly, "I'll rest at the top." He offered his arm with a formality that was entirely, characteristically disingenuous.

I took it. My ribs and my pride had a brief internal discussion and reached a compromise.

The climb through the rift took twenty minutes. The summit, when we reached it, was a landscape of such complete transformation that for a moment I simply stood in it and let my mind catch up to my eyes. The fused-glass ring was exactly sixty-three feet in diameter—I paced it. The launch crater at its center had a depth of approximately four feet and a temperature, even now, that made the air above it shimmer. The Aretia stratigraphy was visible in the sheared cliff face to the north: three centuries of engineered stone, and beneath it the first glimpse of something older, a carved lintel in a style of masonry I had never seen in any archive and recognized instantly from the image Advast had shown me in racial memory.

The city was real. The city was real, and intact, and waiting below the mountain with all the patience of something that has been waiting for a very long time and has no particular objection to waiting a little longer.

I opened the field notebook. I began to document.

Xaden sat on a piece of rubble at the crater's edge, tilted his face up to the sun, and rested. He looked, in that moment, less like the most dangerous person I had ever met and more like someone who has been carrying a very heavy thing for a very long time and has set it down in a patch of sunlight for just a few minutes. I looked at him for longer than was strictly necessary for documentation purposes.

Then I looked back at the carved lintel and kept writing.

Two hours later, when I had filled eleven pages with measurements, structural observations, stratigraphic notation, and the first complete written account of what had occurred here in the past three days—every word of it, including her—I reached the final entry.

I wrote: The portal closed at dawn on the fourth day. The sky held.

I wrote: She is not gone. She is resting. The distinction matters.

I closed the notebook.

Xaden opened one eye. "Done?"

"For now." I looked out over the valley—at the ruined city and the surviving wards and the thin, recovering pulse of the ley lines and the improbable, obstinate green of Idris at the valley's edge. "The official record is going to say we defeated the Black Hole King using a Supernova-Rune and a Primary Anchor discharge. Which is true. As far as it goes."

"And the unofficial record?"

I pressed my palm to my sternum, where the scar of the recognition-filament was still warm. "Is here. And here." I tapped the notebook. "And will be in the Archive, in the restricted annex, in a file marked 'cartographer's error,' where the right person will find it when they need to."

Xaden looked at me for a long moment. Then he said: "Your father would have approved."

It was the most precise thing anyone had said to me in three days. I didn't trust my voice with a response, so I simply reached over and put my hand in his.

We sat on the summit of Basgiath in the morning sun, with the valley laid out below us like a map of everything that had almost been lost and hadn't been, and we stayed there until the distant sound of dragon wings broke the silence from the south—the first of the Skystrikers, coming to find out what was left.

Chapter Thirty-Five

They came in waves.

First the Skystrikers—forty riders, General Lilith Sorrengail at their head, her charcoal leathers coated in the fine ash of three days of border combat, her expression when she landed in the valley and saw the blue-glass city and the shattered summit doing something I had never once in my life seen my mother's expression do: it faltered.

She found me on the valley floor. She looked at me for a long moment—at the scorched leathers, the field-notebook, the careful way I was holding my left side where the Healing rune was still working.

"You're alive," she said.

"I made the calculations," I said. "They were accurate."

She put her hand on my shoulder. It lasted approximately two seconds. For her, that was an eternity. I understood, in those two seconds, that it was the closest she knew how to come to the thing she actually meant, and I accepted it as such.

"The King," she said.

"Gone."

"The Void seam."

"Closed."

"The wards."

"Holding. The core needs resupply within ten days. I have a full structural and ley line assessment in—" I held up the notebook.

She took it. Read the first three pages with the focused, vertical attention of someone who processes information the way a blade processes resistance. Stopped at the fourth page.

"What is Aretia," she said. Not a question. The tone of someone who has encountered a word they recognize from a context they'd sealed away.

"A city," I said. "Sealed beneath the summit by the First Riders. The records were classified above General-rank. I imagine you have clearance."

Her eyes came up from the notebook. She looked at me with an expression I had no existing category for and would spend some time afterwards trying to correctly name. It was somewhere between professional assessment, maternal reckoning, and the particular look of a person who is being forced to revise a very old assumption.

"Your father found a reference," she said. "Twenty years ago. I told him it was a scribal error."

"He thought so too," I said. "He was wrong. So were you."

A pause.

"Yes," she said. "We were." She handed the notebook back. "Finish the record. Full archive standard. Restricted annex." The ghost of something that was almost—almost—a softening moved across her face. "Your father would have wanted it done properly."

"I know," I said. "I'm doing it properly."

She held my gaze for one more moment. Then she turned to begin coordinating the Skystriker assessment teams, her boots finding their familiar, authoritative rhythm across the rubble.

I let out a breath I had been holding for approximately twenty years.

The Silent Brothers arrived next—six of them, eyeless and grey-robed, moving through the blue-glass graveyard with the unhurried attention of beings for whom disaster is primarily a cataloguing problem. They set immediately to assessing the crystallized sections of the city for structural stability and began the long, patient work of reversing the Neptune-God's legacy where reversal was possible and documenting it where it wasn't.

The Fourth Wing riders arrived on the second day—the ones who had been stationed at the outer borders when the tectonic events began. They came in ragged, exhausted, their dragons bearing the minor burns and scale-damage of three days of peripheral combat with the Venin who had tried to press the borders during the chaos. Seeing the valley seemed to break something loose in all of them simultaneously—that specific, physical release of tension that a body can only perform when it finally believes the danger is past.

Ridoc received seventeen separate expressions of relief from his squadmates, which he bore with increasing emotional dishevelment until the seventeenth, at which point he sat down and stopped pretending he was fine, and Rhiannon sat beside him without comment and remained there for approximately an hour, and this was, I noted without writing it down, the most effective Silent Brother maneuver I had ever witnessed.

Brennan was found by the Third Wing riders on the second morning, alive and mobile and bearing the specific expression of a man who has been officially dead for several years and is uncertain of the administrative process for reversing this. The reunion with our mother was witnessed by approximately forty people and documented by none of them, which was correct.

The valley slowly, effortfully, incompletely began to resemble something that could be rebuilt.

On the third day, Xaden found me in what remained of the archive room—the section of the library that had survived, its wards intact, its shelves holding with the particular stubbornness of well-built things that have decided not to fall down. I was cataloguing. I had been cataloguing for most of the past thirty hours, with breaks for sleep enforced by Rhiannon's telekinesis and meals enforced by Ridoc's persistence.

"The Silent Brothers want the summit sealed," he said, from the doorway.

"I know." I didn't look up from the index I was compiling. "I already spoke to them. They agreed to a six-week documentation window before any structural work begins."

"You negotiated with the Silent Brothers."

"I made a logical case."

"The Silent Brothers don't speak."

"They communicate. I used the Scribe-link." I turned a page. "They were receptive to the argument that sealing the site before full documentation was epistemically irresponsible."

He was quiet for a moment. I heard him move into the room—the specific quality of silence that is Xaden navigating a space with his shadows drawn close, attentive, the unconscious proprioception of someone whose power is also a kind of perception.

He leaned against the shelf beside my table. I kept writing.

"Elara," he said.

"Mm."

"Put the notebook down for a moment."

"I'm in the middle of a—"

"Elara."

Something in the way he said it. Not urgent. Not alarmed. The particular tone that is none of those things and lands with more weight than all of them. I put the notebook down and turned to look at him.

He looked tired in the way that goes deeper than sleep—the tiredness of someone who has been holding themselves at maximum tension for a very long time and is only now, in the relative quiet, feeling the cost of it. His shadows were still. The Mark on his neck had faded from combat-dark to its ordinary depth. His eyes were on me with that specific, unguarded attention that he deployed rarely and with the full knowledge of its effect.

"When this is documented," he said. "When the record is complete. When the Silent Brothers have their six weeks and the archive has its restricted file and the official account is submitted and approved." He paused. "What do you want to do?"

I had not thought about this. There had been no room to think about it—the days since the King's fall had been pure logistics, pure recovery, pure documentation, the urgent present tense of survival work that leaves no space for the future. The question landed in the middle of me with a weight I hadn't expected.

What did I want to do.

I looked at the archive around me—the shelves of surviving records, the indexed history of a world that had nearly ended and hadn't, the incomplete annals of a mountain with a buried city beneath it and a story that would take decades to fully tell. I looked at my notebook, eleven pages of the most important documentation I would ever produce. I thought about the carved lintel in the sheared summit face, the masonry style I had never seen in any existing archive, the city intact and waiting.

I thought about the warm scar on my sternum where the recognition-filament had been.

"I want to go down," I said. "Into Aretia. I want to document what's there." I paused. "And I want you to come with me."

The corner of his mouth moved. Not a smile exactly—the specific, restrained precursor to one. "Into an unmapped underground city of unknown extent beneath a mountain that recently exploded."

"The structural assessment indicates the lower chambers are stable."

"Your structural assessment."

"My structural assessments are accurate." I held his gaze. "There are records down there that predate anything in this library. There are answers to questions my father spent his entire life asking. And there is—" I stopped. Pressed my hand briefly to my sternum. "There is something down there that she was sealed to protect. And I think it's time someone found out what it was."

Xaden looked at me for a long moment.

"When do we go?" he said.

"After the six weeks," I said. "After the documentation window. After I've finished the index and submitted the official account and made sure the restricted file is correctly catalogued."

"Of course." He reached over and closed the notebook for me, gently, with one hand. "But not tonight."

"The index—"

"Will be there in the morning." He held out his hand. "You have three broken ribs that are eighty percent healed, you haven't slept in a continuous block for five days, and Advast is circling the valley sending me pointed messages about your caloric intake."

I looked at his hand. I looked at the notebook. I performed a quick assessment of priorities and reached a conclusion.

I took his hand.

"The index will be there in the morning," I agreed.

We left the archive, and the valley was quiet around us, and above it the stars were exactly where they were supposed to be.

Chapter Thirty-Six

Six weeks later, I stood at the lip of the Aretia chasm with a Stele in one hand and a fully charged field-kit of Adamas in the other and more questions than I had ever held in my head at one time in my life.

That is saying something.

The summit had been documented to archive standard. Every measurement, every stratigraphic layer, every notation about the fused-glass ring and the launch crater and the carved lintel—all of it submitted, reviewed by the Silent Brothers, countersigned by the Head Librarian's office, and filed in the restricted annex under a reference code that would be findable by the right person and invisible to the wrong one. The official account of the Black Hole King's defeat was in the public record. The unofficial account was in eleven pages of a scorched field-notebook, preserved in oilskin, lodged in the restricted annex directly behind the official account with a note in my handwriting that read: See also: cartographer's error, Third Age, ref. Aretia, N.

My father would have found it in approximately four minutes. I had confidence in the eventual reader.

The chasm itself had been secured—the structural assessment confirmed stable chambers to a depth of two hundred feet, and the Silent Brothers had run a preliminary ward-survey of the first three levels and found the original First Rider wards largely intact, dormant but coherent, waiting for an authorized Stele to reactivate them. The kind of wards that don't degrade. The kind that were built by people who intended what they sealed to remain sealed for as long as it needed to be.

They had been built by people who understood that sealed did not mean destroyed. That sleeping did not mean gone. That the right moment was worth waiting for.

I understood that too, now. I had a scar on my sternum that had taught it to me.

"Ready?" Xaden asked, from beside me.

I looked down into the chasm—into the dark, which was not actually dark because I had rigged a series of Illumination runes along the first hundred feet of descent and the Adamas-light threw the walls into sharp relief, revealing the carved stone of Aretia's uppermost level: columns, intact. Archways, intact. The lintel I had documented from the summit face, now fully visible, bearing an inscription in the script of the First Riders that I had spent three of the past six weeks translating and had rendered, provisionally, as: We seal this in trust, not in condemnation. What rests here rests in love.

Rhiannon and Ridoc were already on the rope descent—Ridoc narrating his concerns about the structural integrity of the first archway to no one in particular, Rhiannon ignoring him with the practiced efficiency of long familiarity. Brennan was below them both, his silver eyes providing their own light, one hand trailing the ancient stonework as he descended.

Advast was on the summit above us, too large for the chasm, his void-black presence a warm constant at the edge of the bond. I will be here, he had said, when we'd discussed the logistics. I am always here. And then, after a pause: She is also here, wildfire. Not in a way you can see. But in a way you can record.

I had written that down too.

I looked at Xaden. He was watching me with the patient, attentive stillness of someone who has learned that the most important moments are rarely the ones that announce themselves.

"Ready," I said.

I stepped off the edge.

The descent took eight minutes—controlled, careful, my Stele tracing a stabilizing line along the rope to keep my joints from bearing full load. The Adamas-light grew warmer as we went deeper, catching on surfaces that had not seen light in centuries and reflecting it back in colors I had no names for yet but intended to find names for, or invent them if none existed. The air changed from cold and thin to something older and more complex—the specific atmospheric chemistry of a sealed space, mineral-rich, the trace signatures of materials I would need to have analyzed properly but could already tell were not in any existing index.

The floor of the uppermost chamber arrived under my boots with a solidity that felt, after everything, almost shocking. I let go of the rope and stood in the city of Aretia, in a space that had last been stood in by the First Riders, and was quiet for a moment.

It was large. Not the word—vast was closer, cathedral closer still, and even then the scale exceeded the vocabulary. The ceiling was supported by columns fifty feet high, carved from the mountain's own stone and inlaid with Adamas in patterns I recognized immediately as derivations of the root-stroke I had etched into the shard—the same fractal geometry, rendered in permanent heavenly silver, covering every column from base to capital. The effect, with the Illumination runes active, was of standing inside a lattice of living light.

In the center of the chamber, on a raised dais of the same unknown masonry as the lintel, was a door.

Not an archway. A door—a single slab of material that was not stone and not metal and not Adamas, though it shared properties with all three. It was approximately twelve feet tall and perfectly sealed, and around its frame was carved, in the script of the First Riders, a text I recognized.

I had translated it three weeks ago from a fragment in my father's library. He had marked it provenance unknown, possibly apocryphal.

It read: Here we place what cannot be destroyed. Here we place what must not be lost. Here we place the record of the first fire, the first bond, the first word spoken between a rider and a dragon at the beginning of the world. Reader: if you have come this far, you were meant to.

I stood in front of the door for a long time.

Xaden appeared beside me, looked at the inscription, looked at me.

"Can you read it?"

"Yes." I traced the letters with one finger, not quite touching. "It's the First Rider script. I've been translating it for three weeks." I turned to him. "Xaden. This isn't just a city. This is an archive. Aretia was their archive—the First Riders'. Everything they knew. Everything they learned. Everything they wanted to survive them."

"And the Progenitor," he said slowly, "was sealed here to protect it."

"She wasn't a guard," I said. The word came out with a conviction that surprised me—immediate, certain, the feeling of a translation that has finally resolved into its correct meaning. "She was a curator. She was sealed here to keep the record intact until someone came who could be trusted with it."

In my chest, the warm scar pulsed once. Steady. Constant.

I pressed my palm to it.

Then I raised my Stele, pressed it to the lock on the door—a single depression in the unknown material, exactly the size and shape of a Stele's tip—and drew one stroke.

The root-stroke of the Supernova geometry. The same mark I had etched into the shard. The same mark that was inlaid in the columns behind me, that was carved into the lintel above, that was threaded through the architecture of this entire city like a signature.

The door opened.

The light that came through it was not Adamas-light and not Illumination-rune light and not the white fire of the Progenitor's eyes. It was the specific, sourceless, warm light of something that has been waiting in a sealed space for a very long time to be let out, and is not surprised that the wait is over, and is not in a hurry, and is simply, quietly, glad.

I stepped through the door.

I opened my notebook.

I began, at the beginning, to write everything down.
